Exploring Art's History of Movement

Vincent Van Gogh at The Met, 1885-2017


When we visit a renowned piece of art in a museum's galleries, we may not always consider the path it traveled to arrive at this destination. Prominent artwork can be bought, sold, traded, and put on display across the far reaches of our world throughout its history - or it can be hidden away in a private collection or in storage. Studying this history of movement can help us understand how the world has interacted with a specific piece of art, giving us clues to its cultural impact and influence on regions, eras, and movements in the broader artistic context.

Vincent Van Gogh created hundreds of paintings in his troubled life, and although he would not enjoy the success of his work during his lifetime, his work has been profoundly influential in Western art. The Met has 18 of his masterpiece landscapes, portraits, and still lifes in their collection. In The Migration of Art, we explore how these paintings traversed the globe, unearthing each nook and cranny of its travels through ownership changes and exhibitions. Our visualization, inspired by E.J. Marey's 1885 graphical train schedule, shows a detailed timeline of movement for painting that provides a visual overview while allowing for deeper exploration.
